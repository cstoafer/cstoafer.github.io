HBT-EP Overview

The driving goal of the HBT-EP experiment is to study plasma physics related to nuclear fusion energy. We investigate plasma instabilities that limit the performance of plasma confinement devices called Tokamaks. Our goal is to understand, suppress, and control these instabilities to improve plasma performance.

My Projects


Rotation Studies with a Biased Probe

The rotation of plasma modes (instabilities) plays a key role in stabilizing them. I aim to understand the physics causing this rotation on HBT-EP and to control it with a biased probe, which applies a torque to the plasma. Rotation is measured in two ways: using magnetic sensors to measure mode rotation and using a Mach probe to measure plasma rotation. This research is also related to performing feedback control of these plasma instabilities.
Key achievements:

  • Instrumented bias probe and incorporated it into the HBT-EP feedback control system.
  • Developed and maintainted data analysis code for measuring mode rotation and typical plasma parameters.
  • Setup Mach probe for measuring plasma rotation.
Thomson Scattering Diagnostic

I implemented a multi-point Thomson scattering diagnostic on HBT-EP. The system uses a high power laser to probe the plasma temperature and density through the interaction with the electrons (Thomson scattering). This new system allows 10 spatial location measurements within the plasma, upgraded from our previous single spatial point system.
Key achievements:

  • Designed, prototyped, and validated new collection system including lens and fiber optic array.
  • Built and aligned new laser system.
  • Developed and implemented automated data analysis for incorporation of measurements into the HBT-EP database.
Atomic Emission Spectroscopy

I setup a spectrometer, which is currently used to look at deuterium line emission (the D-alpha line) because we primarily study deuterium plasmas. This provides information about plasma breakdown, plasma interactions with neutral particles, plasma interactions with the walls, and disruptions (loss of plasma confinement).
